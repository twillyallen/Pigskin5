2026 NFL Cap Casualties: Stars Who Could Be Cut This Offseason

By Twillyallen | March 2026 | 8 min read

Every March, the NFL's salary cap forces teams into painful decisions. Players who were celebrated signings two or three years ago become financial liabilities. Veterans who gave everything to a franchise get released not because of poor performance but because of cap math that simply doesn't work anymore. These cap casualties are one of the most jarring aspects of professional football — a reminder that the business of the game operates independently of loyalty, history, or sentiment.

The 2026 offseason is no different. Teams across the league are running through their cap numbers and identifying contracts that no longer make sense given their current roster construction. Here's a look at the kinds of situations that produce cap casualties and what it means for the players and teams involved.

How Cap Casualties Happen

Understanding cap casualties requires a basic understanding of how NFL contracts work. Most big contracts include signing bonuses that are prorated across the life of the deal for cap purposes. When a team cuts a player before his contract expires, any remaining prorated bonus money accelerates into the current year's cap — a "dead cap" charge that the team absorbs without getting any player production in return.

This dead cap reality means teams must calculate the true cost of a cut. Releasing a player with $20 million remaining on his deal might save $10 million in cap space while generating $5 million in dead cap — a net savings of $5 million. Whether that math makes sense depends on whether the player is still producing at a level worth $10 million in salary, or whether the team would be better served putting that money elsewhere.

The teams most likely to generate cap casualties are those that signed multiple large contracts in the same offseason several years ago and are now dealing with the simultaneous back-loading of those deals. It's a well-documented pattern in cap management — teams paper over short-term cap problems by pushing money into future years, then eventually face a reckoning when those future years arrive.

Positions Most Affected

Wide receivers are cut more frequently than almost any other position group in cap casualty season. The position commands premium contracts, but receiver production tends to decline earlier than contracts anticipate. A receiver who signed a four-year deal as a legitimate WR1 at age 28 often becomes a declining WR2 by year three, leaving teams paying top dollar for diminishing production. The cap math rarely works out in the team's favor.

Defensive backs face similar dynamics. Cornerbacks and safeties who signed large deals in their late 20s often see their coverage ability decline faster than their contracts expire. Teams that paid market rate for a player's peak years frequently find themselves paying above-market rates for his decline phase. The result is a steady stream of veteran defensive backs hitting free agency each spring after being released by teams unable or unwilling to absorb their remaining cap charges.

Offensive linemen represent a different type of cap casualty challenge. The position ages more gracefully than skill positions, but linemen who absorbed significant injuries during their contracts often find teams reluctant to count on them going forward. An offensive tackle who was worth $15 million per year healthy becomes a very different proposition after a serious knee or ankle injury, even if he appears to have recovered fully.

What Cap Casualties Mean for Free Agency

Cap casualties don't just affect the teams releasing players — they reshape the free agency market for every team. When a recognizable veteran name gets cut, teams looking for help at that position suddenly have an option they didn't anticipate. These players often sign for below-market value because they're entering free agency unexpectedly rather than as planned free agents with leverage.

Smart teams monitor cap casualties closely and move quickly when a player they've identified gets released. The window between a cap casualty being cut and signing elsewhere is often very short — within 24 to 48 hours for players in genuine demand. Teams that have done their homework on potential casualties before they happen are positioned to act immediately rather than scrambling to evaluate a player on the fly.

The Human Side of Cap Casualties

It's easy to discuss cap casualties in purely financial terms, but the human dimension is worth acknowledging. These are real players who committed to teams, moved their families, built relationships with coaches and teammates, and in many cases played through pain to honor their contracts. Being released for financial reasons rather than performance reasons carries a particular sting that pure free agency doesn't.

The players who handle cap casualty releases best are those who reframe the experience quickly. Dwelling on the injustice of the situation — however understandable that reaction is — doesn't help a player find a new team or prepare himself to perform at a high level when he does. The veterans who bounce back most successfully after being cut are typically the ones who channel their frustration into preparation and arrive at their new teams with something to prove.

For fans, cap casualties are a frustrating but essential feature of the modern NFL. Understanding why they happen makes them easier to accept, even when a beloved player gets moved on from. The teams that manage their cap most effectively consistently find ways to retain their core players while still creating the flexibility to improve. That balance is genuinely difficult to achieve and worthy of appreciation when teams get it right.
