NFL Draft History: First Overall Picks That Changed Football

Published December 2024 | 10 min read

The NFL Draft represents hope, potential, and the promise of franchise transformation. No selection carries more weight than the first overall pick, where teams pin their futures on a single player. Some first overall picks become Hall of Famers who justify the hype. Others become cautionary tales that haunt franchises for years. Understanding this history reveals patterns about player evaluation, team building, and the inherent uncertainty of predicting success at the professional level.

The Evolution of the NFL Draft

The NFL conducted its first draft in 1936, primarily to promote parity and prevent wealthier teams from monopolizing talent. Jay Berwanger of the University of Chicago was selected first overall by the Philadelphia Eagles, though he never played professional football, choosing business over the sport. This inauspicious beginning foreshadowed the uncertainty that would characterize first picks for decades to come.

Early drafts often produced surprising results by modern standards. Information was limited, scouting was rudimentary, and teams frequently made selections based on regional appeal or limited observation. Many top picks came from nearby universities, as teams sought to capitalize on local fan interest rather than prioritizing pure talent evaluation.

The draft evolved significantly in the television era. By the 1980s, it had become a major media event. ESPN began broadcasting the draft in 1980, and coverage has expanded dramatically since then. Today, the draft spans three days, features prime-time coverage of early rounds, and generates endless analysis and speculation. The first overall pick has become one of the most scrutinized decisions in professional sports.

Legendary First Overall Picks

Some first overall selections lived up to every expectation and then some. Peyton Manning, selected by the Indianapolis Colts in 1998, exemplifies first pick success. Manning spent 14 seasons in Indianapolis, winning a Super Bowl, earning five MVP awards, and revolutionizing quarterback play with his pre-snap adjustments and audibles. His selection over Ryan Leaf, another highly touted quarterback, demonstrates the importance of character evaluation alongside physical talent.

Troy Aikman's selection by the Dallas Cowboys in 1989 proved equally franchise-altering. Aikman led the Cowboys to three Super Bowl victories in the 1990s, anchoring one of the NFL's greatest dynasties. His accuracy, leadership, and ability to perform in crucial moments validated Jerry Jones' commitment to building around an elite quarterback.

John Elway's 1983 selection by the Baltimore Colts created immediate drama when he refused to play for them, forcing a trade to the Denver Broncos. Despite the controversy, Elway justified his status as a generational talent, leading the Broncos to five Super Bowl appearances and winning back-to-back championships to close his career. His combination of arm strength, mobility, and competitive fire made him one of football's all-time greats.

The Quarterback Question

Teams selecting first overall face enormous pressure to find a franchise quarterback. The position's importance has only increased in the modern pass-heavy NFL, making quarterback evaluation the most critical skill in team building. Success stories like Peyton Manning, Eli Manning, and Matthew Stafford demonstrate that the right quarterback can transform a franchise's trajectory for over a decade.

However, quarterback busts at the top of the draft carry devastating consequences. JaMarcus Russell, selected first by the Oakland Raiders in 2007, represents the quintessential cautionary tale. Despite possessing elite physical tools, Russell lacked the work ethic and dedication necessary for NFL success. He lasted just three seasons, completing barely 50 percent of his passes and throwing more interceptions than touchdowns.

The 2021 draft class illustrates the unpredictability of quarterback evaluation. Trevor Lawrence was selected first overall by the Jacksonville Jaguars amid comparisons to Peyton Manning as a "can't miss" prospect. While Lawrence has shown promise, his development has been inconsistent, reminding us that even the most heralded prospects face challenges adapting to professional football.

Positional Value and Draft Strategy

Teams must balance selecting the best player available against addressing positional needs. Defensive players selected first overall often face higher bust rates than quarterbacks, partly because their impact is harder to measure and partly because offensive schemes can neutralize even elite defensive players.

Myles Garrett's selection by the Cleveland Browns in 2017 shows how a dominant defensive player can still provide exceptional value. Garrett has become one of the NFL's premier pass rushers, earning multiple Pro Bowl selections and justifying his status as a cornerstone franchise player. His consistency and production demonstrate that defensive players can succeed as first overall picks when properly evaluated.

Running backs have fallen out of favor as first overall picks as teams have recognized the position's diminishing value in the modern NFL. The last running back selected first was Ki-Jana Carter in 1995, a pick that resulted in disappointment due to injuries. The shift away from running backs reflects broader strategic evolution as teams prioritize passing offense and defensive line play.

Infamous Draft Busts

Some first overall picks become synonymous with failure, serving as warnings about the risks of draft evaluation. Ryan Leaf, selected second overall in 1998 after San Diego traded up, is often mentioned alongside JaMarcus Russell as the biggest quarterback bust in history. Leaf's combination of poor performance, bad attitude, and off-field problems made him a cautionary tale about character evaluation.

Tony Mandarich, selected second overall in 1989, was touted as the best offensive line prospect ever. He failed spectacularly with the Green Bay Packers, lacking the work ethic and dedication to succeed at the professional level. The pick looks even worse in retrospect because the Packers passed on Hall of Famers Barry Sanders, Derrick Thomas, and Deion Sanders to select Mandarich.

The Trade-Up Phenomenon

Teams without the first pick often trade significant assets to move up and select their chosen prospect. These trades represent enormous gambles that can make or break franchises. The Rams' trade up to select Jared Goff first overall in 2016 cost them multiple first-round picks, and while Goff showed promise, he ultimately proved insufficient for championship contention.

Conversely, the Chiefs' trade up to select Patrick Mahomes tenth overall in 2017 demonstrates the rewards of aggressive quarterback acquisition. Although not a first overall pick, Mahomes' selection via trade illustrates how teams must be willing to pay a premium for elite quarterback prospects. His immediate success and MVP-caliber play justified every asset Kansas City surrendered.

Modern Draft Philosophy

Contemporary first overall picks benefit from unprecedented evaluation resources. Teams employ advanced analytics, psychological testing, medical examinations, and extensive background checks. The combine, pro days, and private workouts provide multiple opportunities to assess prospects. Despite these advantages, busts still occur, reminding us that predicting human performance remains an inexact science.

The rookie wage scale, implemented in 2011, reduced the financial risk of first overall picks. Previously, top picks received massive guaranteed contracts before playing a single snap, creating situations where busts crippled team salary caps. The new system allows teams to evaluate players on rookie contracts before committing to long-term extensions, reducing but not eliminating the risk of high draft picks.

Recent First Overall Picks

The last decade of first overall picks shows the position's continued importance in the quarterback-driven NFL. Kyler Murray, Baker Mayfield, and Joe Burrow all went first overall, with varying degrees of success. Burrow's immediate impact, leading the Cincinnati Bengals to a Super Bowl appearance in just his second season, demonstrates how the right quarterback can accelerate a franchise's timeline.

Bryce Young's selection by the Carolina Panthers in 2023 represents the latest chapter in this ongoing story. Despite concerns about his size, Young's processing speed, accuracy, and leadership convinced Carolina to trade up and make him their franchise cornerstone. His development will be scrutinized intensely, as it should be for any first overall pick.

Lessons from Draft History

First overall pick history teaches us several lessons. Physical tools matter, but character, work ethic, and football intelligence often separate success from failure. Teams must balance immediate needs against long-term value, resist pressure to reach for positional scarcity, and maintain conviction in their evaluations despite media and fan pressure.

The draft remains unpredictable despite advances in evaluation. Injuries, scheme fit, coaching changes, and personal circumstances all influence whether a prospect succeeds or fails. The best organizations acknowledge this uncertainty while maximizing their chances through thorough evaluation and player development.

Understanding first overall pick history enhances appreciation for the NFL Draft's drama and significance. These selections represent hope for struggling franchises and anxiety for front offices gambling their careers on a single decision. Whether these players become Hall of Famers or cautionary tales, they all share the burden of immense expectations that comes with being selected first overall.
